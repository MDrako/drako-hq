The end of an era — what's actually happening right now
The Strait of Hormuz is closed — largest oil shock in history
The US-Israel war with Iran (started Feb 2026) disrupted the Strait of Hormuz, which carries 20% of global oil AND 33% of global fertilizer. The World Bank called it the largest supply disruption in the history of the global oil market. Brent crude jumped to $105/barrel — up 44% since the war began. This is the reason inflation re-accelerated, your mortgage rates went back up, and the Fed stopped cutting.
UAE exits OPEC — effective May 1, 2026
This is historic. The UAE — one of OPEC's biggest producers — officially left the cartel. They're now pricing oil in multiple currencies, not just dollars, and cutting bilateral deals directly with China. This is a visible signal that the old dollar-oil-Treasury system is fracturing. Saudi Arabia reportedly needs $170/barrel to break even with current obligations but the paper market is suppressing that price. The OPEC cartel's grip is weakening fast.
The Buffett Indicator is flashing red
The "Adjusted Buffett Indicator" — market cap divided by GDP minus federal debt — is at levels seen only during the 2000 dot-com crash and the 2021 peak. The market is "pricing in perfection" while the real world has closed trade routes, depleted US military stockpiles (China controls rare earths needed to rebuild precision missile inventory), and a monetary system shifting under everyone's feet. This is not the time to be concentrated in one bet.
The two lose-lose scenarios for the global economy
Scenario A — Iran breaks: Oil spikes to $130–$170, global recession follows as consumer spending collapses under energy costs. Scenario B — Supply chains break first: Food and fertilizer shortages cause a massive inflation spike (fertilizer already up 31%, food prices surging globally), triggering stagflation and recession. Both scenarios end in pain. The question is only sequencing and severity.
The 10-year Treasury at 4.4% is a geopolitical tripwire
Every time the 10-yr yield hits 4.4%, the US blinks
This is one of the most important things in the video. The US government is trapped: it needs to keep bond yields low or the interest payments on its $35 trillion debt become unmanageable. Every time the 10-yr Treasury approaches 4.4%, markets panic, the bond market sells off, and the US government de-escalates military rhetoric to calm investors. Foreign policy is being dictated by bond yields.
The trap: print or not print
If the US prints money to stabilize the bond market and keep rates down — commodity inflation explodes further and the dollar loses more reserve status. If the US doesn't print — it can't afford its debt payments and risks a fiscal crisis. This is not a problem with an easy solution. This is why gold is at $4,500 and central banks are buying it aggressively. They're hedging against both outcomes.
The petrodollar system is unwinding — what this means
The 1974 petrodollar deal expired in 2024
Since 1974, Saudi Arabia and Gulf states priced oil in US dollars and recycled those profits into US Treasury bonds. This created artificial global demand for dollars and Treasuries — it's why the US could run massive deficits without the dollar collapsing. That secret agreement expired in 2024. Without a "hard" anchor, the dollar's reserve status depends entirely on trust — and trust is eroding.
The GCC's $2 trillion leverage play — dollar swap lines
The Gulf Cooperation Council holds over $2 trillion in US assets. The UAE used the implicit threat of a "disorderly sale" of those assets — and a pivot to the Chinese Yuan — to force the US Treasury to provide "dollar swap lines." This gives the UAE cheap dollar liquidity without needing to hold Treasuries. Translation: the US paid ransom to prevent a bond market crash.
De-dollarization is accelerating — the 2022 Russia moment
When the US froze $300 billion of Russian sovereign assets in 2022, every central bank on earth got the memo: holding dollars is a political risk, not just a financial one. Since then, central banks have been rotating out of US Treasuries and into gold at the fastest pace in 50 years. The dollar's share of global reserves has dropped from ~71% in 2001 to the 50s% range today — and it's still falling.
China's parallel financial system is being built right now
China has secured direct oil trade deals with the UAE priced in Yuan. They're building alternative payment systems outside of SWIFT. More commodity deals — oil, minerals, food — are being priced in Yuan every month. China is the primary geopolitical winner of this transition because they produce physical goods and are positioning themselves as the alternative to the dollar system. This is a decade-long shift, not a quarter-long trade.
What dollar weakness means for YOUR money
Dollar weakness = hard assets go up
Gold, oil, real estate, commodities — all priced in dollars. When the dollar weakens, these go up in dollar terms even if the underlying asset doesn't change. This is why owning real assets is your best hedge. You don't need to be a currency trader — you just need exposure.
Dollar weakness = international stocks outperform
Non-US stocks outperformed the S&P by the widest margin since 2009 in 2025. When you earn in dollars but assets are globally diversified, dollar depreciation makes international holdings worth more in your currency. India, Southeast Asia, and parts of Europe are worth watching.
Dollar weakness = your debt gets cheaper in real terms
If you have fixed-rate debt (mortgage, car loan) and inflation runs hot, you're repaying that debt with cheaper future dollars. This is actually in your favor as a borrower — as long as your income keeps up with or exceeds inflation. Your fixed mortgage rate is a hedge against inflation if your earning power grows.

The numbers that keep Kiyosaki up at night
$39 trillion on-balance sheet — $250 trillion off-balance sheet
The official national debt is $39T. But when you add unfunded liabilities — Social Security, Medicare, Medicaid, pension obligations — the number balloons to an estimated $250 trillion. This is the debt that doesn't show up in headlines but will define the next 30 years of American fiscal policy. There is no mathematically honest way to pay this without either massive inflation, default, or radical spending cuts.
$1 trillion every 100 days — the printing pace
The US is adding approximately $1 trillion to its national debt every 100 days. To put that in scale: if you spent $1 per minute, it would take 32,500 years to spend $1 trillion. The US is doing that in 100 days. This is not sustainable. At some point the bond market stops accepting this — and when that happens, the adjustment is violent and fast.
The average 401(k) is under $500,000 — a retirement crisis brewing
The median American 401(k) is nowhere near enough to sustain a 20–30 year retirement in an inflationary environment. Most boomers are not financially prepared for the retirement they expect. This is why Kiyosaki argues that traditional retirement advice — "save in a 401k, buy mutual funds" — is systematically flawed for this era. The money loses purchasing power while it sits in paper assets.
Kiyosaki's personal strategy: $1.2 billion in debt as a WEAPON
Kiyosaki personally carries $1.2 billion in real estate debt. His logic: inflation erodes debt. If you borrow at fixed rates and inflation runs hot, you're paying back with cheaper future dollars. The bank takes the real loss. The asset (real estate) appreciates with inflation. The tenant pays the mortgage. This is the "rich dad" playbook — use debt as leverage on income-producing real assets, not to buy depreciating liabilities.
The "end of empire" historical parallel
1956 Suez Crisis ended the British Empire — 2026 Hormuz mirrors it
In 1956, Britain and France tried to retake the Suez Canal from Egypt. The US forced them to stand down. That moment publicly exposed that Britain could no longer project power without US financial backing — and the British Empire's dominance effectively ended. Kiyosaki draws a direct parallel: the US trying to control the Strait of Hormuz while being militarily stretched, financially depleted, and facing a rising China challenger mirrors 1956 almost exactly.
The stock market setup: "yanked at the top"
Kiyosaki's most alarming prediction: the market is being allowed to run to all-time highs specifically to give insiders an exit before a sharp reversal. Boomers with their life savings in 401(k)s — fully allocated to stocks at the top — get crushed and lose their retirement security. Whether you believe the conspiratorial framing or not, the mechanical risk is real: a highly concentrated, overvalued market + a generation of retirees forced to sell = painful.

Investment hedges for a China-rising, dollar-declining world
Copper — COPX ETF Infrastructure megatrend
Copper is the metal of electrification — EVs, power grids, AI data centers, renewable energy infrastructure. China is the world's largest consumer and the world's largest infrastructure builder. Global electrification requires an estimated 50% increase in copper production. Supply is constrained (mines take 10–15 years to develop). This is a decade-long demand story with a supply ceiling. COPX is the copper mining ETF.
Uranium — NUKZ ETF Energy race play
Nuclear energy is experiencing a global renaissance as nations pursue energy independence and AI data centers demand enormous, reliable power. China is building more nuclear reactors than the rest of the world combined. The US, UK, France, and Japan are all extending existing plants and planning new ones. Uranium prices are still well below the levels needed to incentivize new mine supply. NUKZ is the uranium ETF.
Emerging markets — VWO ETF Dollar-decline hedge
VWO (Vanguard Emerging Markets ETF) gives broad exposure to non-US, non-developed-world growth. India, Southeast Asia, Brazil, and others are benefiting from supply chain diversification away from China AND from dollar weakness (their assets look cheaper to international capital). In a multipolar world, pure US concentration is a risk.
China directly — MCHI ETF High risk, geopolitical
MCHI (iShares MSCI China ETF) gives direct China exposure. The thesis is real — China is a beneficiary of de-dollarization and has cheap valuations relative to the US. The risk is also real — US-China tensions, potential sanctions, Taiwan risk, and regulatory unpredictability make this a high-conviction, high-risk position. Small allocation if you believe the multipolar thesis.
